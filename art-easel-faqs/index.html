Art Easel questions answered

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

If you are looking for a clear, honest breakdown of the most common questions about art easels, from types and materials to size and budget, this guide covers what you need to know to pick the right easel for your painting style and space.

What are the main types of art easels?

Most easels fall into a few broad categories. A-frame easels are lightweight, foldable, and great for small to medium canvases. H-frame easels are heavier and offer more stability for larger works. Tabletop easels sit on a desk and work well for watercolor or detail work. French box easels combine a palette and storage box with legs that fold out into a field easel, popular for en plein air painting. There are also studio easels, which are large and robust for professional use, and convertible easels that can be used upright or horizontal for wet media. Understanding these types helps you match the easel to your typical painting setup and space constraints. Most beginners start with an A-frame or a solid tabletop model.

What should I look for in an easel for oil or acrylic painting?

Oil and acrylic painters need a sturdy easel that won’t wobble during vigorous brushwork. Look for a wide base and a strong central mast, typically in hardwood or heavy-duty aluminum. Adjustable canvas tilt is useful for preventing glare and controlling paint flow, and a good mast height lets you work standing or sitting. Many artists prefer an easel with a ratchet or locking mechanism that holds the canvas securely at any angle. A canvas tray that can handle different thicknesses and a built-in palette shelf are bonuses. Avoid very flimsy, budget easels if you work with heavy impasto or large canvases, they can tip or sag.

What kind of easel is best for watercolor?

Watercolor requires a nearly horizontal surface to keep washes from running downward. A tabletop easel with a tilt mechanism that goes close to flat is ideal. Some A-frame easels can also tilt back, but you will usually need a separate board or clip to hold paper securely. Field easels designed for watercolor often have a wooden or plastic backing that supports a watercolor block. If you work at a desk, a minimalist tabletop easel that raises the painting to a comfortable angle is a good choice. Avoid floor easels with a vertical-only lock, as they force the paper vertical, causing drips. A quality watercolor easel will have a simple, positive tilt lock and a smooth adjustability range.

How do I choose an easel for a child or beginner?

Beginners and children benefit from an easel that is easy to set up, light enough to move, and adjustable to their height. Many starter easels come with a chalkboard or dry-erase side, plus a paper roll holder. Look for one with a sturdy plastic or wooden frame that won’t tip over easily, and a tray for supplies. Adjustable legs are important so the easel grows with the child or can be used by different family members. For adult beginners, a simple A-frame easel or a budget-friendly tabletop model is often enough to start. Avoid buying an extremely cheap easel that might break quickly, spending a bit more on a mid-range model ensures a better experience. Portability is a plus if you plan to paint in different rooms or outdoors.

Do I need a floor easel or a tabletop easel?

The answer depends on your workspace and how you like to paint. Floor easels are freestanding and let you work at standing height, which many artists prefer for full-body movement. They require floor space and are heavier. Tabletop easels are compact, fit on a desk, and are good for small studios or apartment dwellers. They limit canvas size but are easy to store. If you primarily paint seated or work small (up to about 18 inches), a tabletop easel may suit you well. If you like to stand, paint large, or switch between canvases during a session, a floor easel is the better investment. Some artists own both: a tabletop for sketching and a floor easel for finished pieces.

What materials are easels made from, and which is better?

Common materials include wood (beech, oak, pine), aluminum, and steel. Wood easels are traditional, stable, and look beautiful, but they are heavier and can dent. Beech is a popular mid-weight hardwood that resists warping. Aluminum easels are lightweight, rust-resistant, and fold compactly, making them great for travel. Steel easels are very heavy duty but also very heavy to move. For indoor studio use with large canvases, a wood easel is often preferred for its warmth and solidity. For outdoor or frequent transport, aluminum is hard to beat. There is no single best material, it is about matching weight, stability, and budget. Many artists find that a high-quality aluminum easel can last just as long as wood if cared for properly.

Frequently asked questions

Can I use a tabletop easel for large canvases?

In general, tabletop easels are designed for canvases up to about 20 inches on the longest side. Attempting a large canvas on a small easel can make it unstable and difficult to work on without tipping. If you paint large, a floor easel is a much safer and more comfortable choice.

How much space do I need for a floor easel?

A typical floor easel needs a clear area roughly 3 feet wide and 3 feet deep, plus extra room for movement and supplies. Allow at least 4 feet of overhead clearance for the mast and canvas. Measure your space before buying, especially if you plan to use it in a corner.

Are adjustable easels worth the extra cost?

Yes, if you plan to switch between canvas sizes or work in different positions (sitting vs standing). Adjustability in height, tilt, and sometimes width gives you flexibility that a fixed easel cannot offer. For a hobbyist who always works at one desk, a simple non-adjustable model may be fine.

What is the difference between a studio easel and a field easel?

A studio easel is large, heavy, and built for permanent indoor use. It often has a massive canvas capacity and wind-up or geared adjustments. A field easel is portable, lightweight, and collapses to a compact size for outdoor painting. Field easels usually sacrifice some stability for portability.

Do I need additional accessories like a palette or canvas holders?

A palette holder or shelf is very useful but not always included. Many easels come with a built-in tray or shelf. Separate accessories like canvas clips, acrylic paint cups, or a stabilizer bar can improve your experience but are not required to start. Check the included components before buying extras.

How do I store an easel when not in use?

Most easels fold flat or collapse partially for storage. A-frame and tabletop easels can be tucked behind a door or under a bed. Floor easels often have folding legs and can stand upright in a closet. Avoid storing them in damp basements or hot attics, as extreme conditions can warp wood or rust metal parts.

In shortChoosing an art easel is about matching your primary medium, available space, and how often you relocate your setup. Start with a mid-range model that offers good adjustability and solid materials, most artists outgrow the cheapest options quickly. Read owner feedback from painters who work in a similar style to yours, and if possible, try adjusting an easel in person to see how confident you feel with its locks and stability. A well-chosen easel becomes a quiet partner in your creative process, not a distraction.
