How to choose the right art easel

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

The best art easel for you depends on your medium, space, and budget. For beginners, a sturdy A‑frame or tabletop easel offers versatility at a reasonable cost. Professionals may prefer a convertible or H‑frame easel for larger canvases and stability.

What type of easel is best for oil painting?

Oil painters typically need an easel that can handle the weight of a wet canvas and stay rock‑steady while you work. A‑frame and H‑frame easels are popular choices: A‑frames fold flat for storage, while H‑frames offer a wider base and more support for heavy canvases. Convertible easels with a horizontal position are also useful for oils because they let you work flat on large pieces. Look for models with adjustable canvas holders and a reliable ratchet or screw mechanism to tilt the canvas forward or back. Many published reviews highlight that a solid wood or heavy‑gauge metal central post prevents wobbling during vigorous brushwork. Owner feedback often notes that a built‑in shelf or tray for palette and brushes is a convenience worth prioritizing.

Should I get a tabletop or floor easel?

Tabletop easels are great if you work in a small apartment or have a dedicated desk space. They are lightweight, easy to store, and usually cost less than floor models, but they limit canvas size to roughly the height of your table. For watercolor or small acrylic studies, a tabletop easel is perfectly adequate and many artists keep one for travel as well. Floor easels give you room to stand back and view your work from a distance, which is important for composition and for larger paintings. They also accommodate a wider range of canvas sizes and often include a palette tray. However, they take up floor space and are heavier to move. If you have a studio or a corner you can dedicate to art, a floor easel is usually the better long‑term investment.

What features matter most for a studio easel?

Stability is the top priority in a studio easel. Look for a wide tripod or H‑shaped base, rubber feet that grip the floor, and a crank‑ or ratchet‑style height adjustment that stays put. Many expert reviews recommend a steel or solid wood central mast for the heaviest canvases, and a canvas tray that rotates horizontally for perspective work. Adjustable tension on the canvas holder is another key feature, it should clamp securely without marring the canvas edge. For a permanent studio setup, consider an easel with casters so you can roll it out of the way. Owner feedback often highlights that a built‑in drawer or shelf for supplies is a nice bonus, but not essential if you have a separate taboret.

How do I choose an easel for a child or beginner?

For a child or beginner, priority should go to ease of use, safety, and value. A simple A‑frame or tabletop easel with a chalkboard or dry‑erase side is a common starting point because it’s forgiving and multifunctional. Many art educators recommend a model with adjustable height so it can grow with the child, look for a range that starts near 30 inches and goes up to at least 50 inches. Avoid heavy, complicated mechanisms that a young artist might struggle to operate. A lightweight aluminum or plastic easel is fine for early exploration, but if the beginner is an adult who plans to paint seriously, a budget‑friendly wooden A‑frame easel offers better stability. Multiple published reviews warn against the cheapest folding models, which can tip over easily with a medium‑sized canvas.

Are aluminum or wood easels better?

Wood easels are classic, stable, and often absorb minor vibrations, which can be helpful for detailed work. They look beautiful in a studio and can last for decades with care. However, they are heavier and can be sensitive to humidity, which may cause joints to loosen over time. Many professional painters prefer wood for its solid feel and the warm aesthetic. Aluminum easels are lightweight, rust‑resistant, and excellent for travel or plein air painting. They fold compactly and often have quick‑release clasps for fast setup. The trade‑off is that very thin aluminum models can feel a bit springy with large canvases, but heavier‑gauge options are just as stable as wood. Owner feedback on aluminum easels praises their portability, but some note that plastic components in the locking mechanisms can wear out faster than metal ones.

What about easels for outdoor sketching?

Plein air painters need an easel that is lightweight, quick to set up, and stable on uneven ground. French‑style box easels combine a storage box, palette, and easel in one unit and are a favorite among outdoor sketchers. They typically have telescoping legs and a central mast that can be tilted, and they pack down into a compact carry case. For extreme portability, a simple aluminum tripod easel with a canvas holder can be enough if you don’t need a built‑in palette. Many published reviews of outdoor easels warn that the budget‑friendly options may wobble in wind, so look for models with spiked feet or a spreader brace. A shoulder strap or backpack case is a worthwhile consideration if you’ll be hiking to your painting spot.

Frequently asked questions

What size easel do I need for a 24x36 canvas?

You’ll need an easel that supports at least that height and width. Most full‑size floor easels can handle canvases up to 48 inches tall, while tabletop easels are usually limited to 20–30 inches. Check the listed maximum canvas height before buying, and consider that a larger canvas also requires a wider base for balance.

Can I use an easel for watercolor painting?

Yes, but you’ll want an easel that lets you tilt your paper nearly flat to control water flow. Many convertible or tabletop easels offer a horizontal position, which is ideal for watercolor. Avoid easels that only hold the canvas upright, as wet washes will run downward.

How much should I spend on a beginner easel?

A decent beginner easel can cost between budget‑friendly and a few hundred dollars. Look for a solidly built wooden A‑frame or an aluminum tripod in the middle of that range. Very cheap easels often lack stability and may frustrate a new artist, so it’s worth spending a bit more for a model that doesn’t wobble.

Do I need a stool with my easel?

Not necessarily, but many artists find a drafting stool or adjustable chair helpful for sessions lasting more than an hour. If you sit, make sure your easel’s canvas center is at eye level when seated. Some easels are designed to be used only while standing, so check the height range if you plan to sit.

Are adjustable easels worth it?

Yes, adjustable height and tilt let you work comfortably in different positions and tackle different canvas sizes. A fixed‑height easel can force poor posture or limit your project scope. Most quality easels offer at least some adjustment, and the extra cost is usually justified by the flexibility.

What is the difference between A‑frame and H‑frame easels?

An A‑frame easel has three legs forming an A shape; it folds flat and is lightweight, but can be less stable with very large canvases. An H‑frame easel has two vertical supports and a central crossbar, resembling an H. It offers greater stability and is better suited for heavy or oversized canvases, but it’s heavier and more expensive.

In shortRemember, the right easel supports your art style and grows with you. Focus on stability, adjustability, and your workspace limits. A well‑chosen easel is a tool that will serve you for years, whether you’re sketching outdoors or finishing a large studio piece.
